Gay people shouldn't be banned from donating blood

Right now, any man who had sex with another man is banned from donating blood. Most LGBTQ organizations view these policies as based on homophobia rather than legitimate medical reasons.

The case right now

Currently, any man who had sex with men (MSM) in the Philippines cannot donate blood. Even in other countries such as the United States of America, Australia, Denmark, and New Zealand, gay men have to wait for months to donate blood.

During the CoVid pandemic, a blood shortage in the US happened which forced hospitals to postpone some surgeries. Yet this wasn't uncommon - blood shortages have happened many times in the past. When vast amounts of people are unable to donate blood, even if they wanted to help, they cannot do so - and this is currently the case with gay men. However, it is important to remember that this ban does not exist for no reason.

Facts and Figures

  1. 1

    MSM are at higher risk of HIV than similar demographics. In the US, 69% of new HIV diagnoses were among gay and bisexual men.

  2. 4

    Many MSM are in monogamous relationships and practice safe sex practices. In fact, only 30% of gay men are in open relationships which makes it far from the norm.

  3. 2

    HIV is still a serious problem with cases rising every year in the Philippines and only slowing down in the Covid-19 pandemic. HIV can be passed through sexual contact and blood transfusions. However, cases due to the latter are rare.

  4. 5

    Every blood donation is tested for diseases regardless of the answer in the questionnaire. In the Philippines, every donation is tested for Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, HIV-1, HIV-2, and syphilis.

  5. 3

    Even the fastest test has a 10 day window where HIV positive patients can test negative. The nucleic acid test (NAT) can tell you if you have HIV 10 to 33 days after exposure while other types of tests can tell it around 18 to 90 days after exposure.

  6. 6

    Many MSM do donate blood regardless of the policy. These men regularly get tested for HIV and practice safe sex to mitigate their risks. They realize that helping other people should not be discriminated against.

Arguments supporting the ban

And why they aren't right

The safety of the blood supply is more important than the feelings of gay men.

While true, blood is almost always tested for diseases including HIV. A polyamorous male with multiple partners is at higher risk for HIV than a monogamous couple yet the former is able to donate. Bans should be based on risk behavior and not on gender identity.

We should focus on getting blood from those who are eligible.

It would still remove a whole lot of good blood from people who want to donate. A gay man with O negative blood (the so-called "universal donor") is unable to donate his blood simply because he's gay.

Tests are not perfect and thus blanket bans may be necessary to ensure safety.

While it’s true that no HIV test is perfect, a blanket ban based on gender identity rather than risk behaviour is discriminatory. Men who had multiple female sex partners can donate blood but are not discriminated against yet are still at elevated risks than similar demographics.

How do I support this advocacy?

Support a ban based on risk behaviour rather than a blanket one based on gender identity.

A blanket ban discriminates against any person in the group who practices safe sexual practices. A man who does not use condoms and has multiple female sex partners is not safer than a gay man in a monogamous relationship because of his sexual orientation.

Support increased and better testing of blood.

Better testing is one of the reasons the ban has been shortened in many countries. Better testing means that we are now able to screen out blood samples faster and cheaper while ensuring the safety of the blood supply from sexually transmitted diseases.
